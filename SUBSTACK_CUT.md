# The Hand of God Problem

### Why product designers need to learn national security

In 1986, Diego Maradona scored the most infamous goal in football history. Argentina vs. England, World Cup quarterfinal. The ball dropped into the box, Maradona rose, and it went in—off a hand the referee didn't see. Later he called it *La Mano de Dios*: the Hand of God.

People still argue about that moment like it's theology. But the interesting question isn't the morality. It's why it became a national myth.

I learned that the hard way. First day of school in Buenos Aires, where my father had just been posted as a diplomat. I had maybe two sentences of Spanish, and recess was a wall of noise I couldn't parse. A kid walked up and asked—in words I could barely decode but whose meaning was unmistakable—whether Maradona's goal was valid. Was I pro-English, or else?

I had nothing. No Spanish. No Maradona. No idea a goal could be a theological event. But to them, this was a rite of passage. It wasn't a debate about rules. It was a belonging test.

Argentina was living in the afterburn of the Falklands War. Defeat, humiliation, grief. In that context, the Hand of God wasn't a petty rule-break. It was revenge. A reset. Not clean, not polite—effective. When it went in, church bells tolled, car horns blared, strangers wept in each other's arms. A ritual—I mean that in a spiritual sense—to take out a curse.

The uncomfortable insight isn't "cheating is good." It's that people unify around capability and will, especially when official channels feel rigged against them. A shared myth can work like a cultural immune response.

I'm a product designer, and that mythology keeps resurfacing when I look at American tech right now. Not because we should cheat. Because we're stuck in a trance—mistaking weakness for virtue while the world moves on without us.

## The trance of performative goodness

There's a peculiar reflex in American tech: the faster someone builds something powerful, the more urgently they perform uncertainty about whether it should exist at all.

Build frontier AI? Immediately signal worry, apologize for the implications, hope you don't "dominate." Control a chokepoint in the stack? Flinch. Downplay. Apologize for leverage.

Caution is wisdom. Performative caution is something else—narrating your own self-handicapping as morality, treating defenselessness as virtue and calling it "principle."

There's a saying: a good man is a very dangerous man who has that under voluntary control. The tech sector has inverted it. We built extraordinary power, then refused to acknowledge it, secure it, or wield it. That's not virtue. That's leaving loaded weapons in unlocked rooms and calling it peace.

## The sword you pretend not to hold

If you're designing AI systems, cloud platforms, social networks, supply chain software—anything that shapes coordination at scale—you are not "just" building a product. You're building dual-use infrastructure. The boundary between "civilian tech" and "national security" didn't dissolve because anyone signed a treaty. It dissolved because software became the terrain.

The other side isn't hiding this. The Pentagon's 2025 report to Congress describes Beijing's vision of future conflict not as a clash of militaries but as a "clash of national systems"—a whole-of-nation mobilization it calls "national total war." China controls roughly 90% of the world's rare-earth processing; a single F-35 contains over 900 pounds of the stuff. Supply chains are the battlefield now.

We are building products in a market. They are building capacity in a system. When those two models collide, the market usually loses.

The simplest way to beat a strong country is to convince it to sabotage itself. Adversaries don't only attack systems; they attack a society's confidence in its right to defend itself. Healthy critique is not subversion—governance questions are real, AI-safety risks are real. The line is between accountability and self-disarmament: between "we should steer this responsibly" and "we should feel guilty for having it." When a culture can't tell the difference, it becomes easy to steer.

## Reading the room like a Protocol officer

I watched the inauguration and saw, in one frame, a cluster of tech leaders—Bezos, Zuckerberg, Pichai, Musk, Cook, Altman—gathered near the center of American political power.

My father worked Protocol in the Foreign Service. Seating charts were his chess. Positions at an inauguration aren't made off-the-cuff; they're composed—for foreign governments—the way a sentence is composed. Putting that stack of CEOs in the frame was a message: AI is a state priority now. It was the equivalent of parading a new tank through Red Square.

The immediate reaction from many: corruption, oligarchy, dystopia.

Mine was different. Look past the user interface of politics to the code underneath. The UI was wealth standing too close to power. The code was capacity—the leaders of the systems that shape global communication, commerce, cloud, and AI, assembled in one shot. Russia can parade tanks. That image represented the capacity to design, build, and coordinate at planetary scale.

If your culture treats its strongest capabilities as moral stains by default, you don't need foreign enemies. You've already done their work.

## The education gap

Here's my actual point. Product designers need national security literacy, because we are already assets in a national security architecture—whether we admit it or not. Not "should be." Already are.

How real intelligence services operate. Supply chain risk. Information operations. The difference between "open by default" and "strategically open."

And here's why it should feel less foreign than it sounds: this is the skill we already have, pointed at a domain we've been avoiding. My first CPO—my father—trained me for it without ever using the words. Discomfort? Good. Ambiguity? Good. Pressure? Good. Product design is the discipline of sitting with messy, adversarial systems without flinching—and shipping anyway. The gap isn't aptitude. It's literacy. Comfort is not a strategy.

## The Hand of God is not an instruction manual

Annie Jacobsen—who spent years interviewing the CIA's paramilitary legends for *Surprise, Kill, Vanish*—tells a story about Billy Waugh and Ric Prado. She asked each of them: if you two had to kill each other, who would win? Of course, each said "me." Then Billy came back to her: "Let me tell you how I would win. I'd cheat. I'd show up before the duel and I'd kill him."

That's not about murder. It's a mindset. The dangerous player isn't the one preparing for a fair duel—it's the one who refuses the duel entirely, who changes the terrain and wins before you recognize what game you're in. Respond only with polite predictability, and you've done half their job for them.

Two guardrails. Strategic cunning without ethics is corruption in a nicer suit—but ethics without strategic cunning is an elaborate form of surrender. And myth-mode can't be permanent: *La Mano de Dios* can carry a nation through an emergency; it cannot govern one. The myth can spark. It can't steer.

Restraint only means something if you're capable of doing otherwise. He who has a sword, and knows how to use it, but keeps it sheathed—he inherits the earth.

Perhaps these are grand conclusions for complex times. Which brings me to the final line—and it's not modesty. It's the indictment.

I am just a product designer.

If *I* can see that we're building dual-use infrastructure while pretending it's "just tech," then none of us has an excuse not to learn the literacy required to steward it.

The trance ends when we accept the sword in our hands.
